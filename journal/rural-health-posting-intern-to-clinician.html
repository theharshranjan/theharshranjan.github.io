Rural Health Posting: A Journey from an Anxious Intern to a Resilient Clinician

Student Contribution · Dr Mahek Jamadar · 8 min read

Stepping into my rural health posting, I carried the mindset of a tertiary care centre. In large tertiary hospitals, we have immediate access to every investigative machine and a superspecialist for almost every disease. Moving from such a setup to a remote area pushed me to rely on my own clinical skills and judgement, without much reliance on diagnostic technology — forcing me to rise above my constraints.

Becoming the Doctor

The rural posting helped me become a complete doctor, fast. In a city hospital, you are one of fifty; in a rural Primary Health Centre (PHC), you are the doctor — the one handling a critical road traffic accident, managing deliveries in the dead of night, attending to every kind of animal bite (snake bite, dog bite, scorpion sting), and responding to medical emergencies such as myocardial infarction and cerebrovascular accidents (CVA). When these emergencies arrived, there was no time for hesitation.

One experience remains etched in my memory: an elderly farmer arrived with sudden, severe chest pain radiating to his left arm, profuse sweating, and breathlessness — a classic presentation of suspected myocardial infarction (MI). We immediately monitored his vital signs, assessed airway, breathing and circulation, established intravenous access, and obtained an ECG. Emergency treatment was initiated as per protocol: oxygen (only when clinically indicated), aspirin, clopidogrel, and a statin, along with appropriate pain relief and supportive care. The nearest higher centre was several kilometres away. After stabilising him, we arranged an urgent referral to a cardiac centre for further management. That day taught me an invaluable lesson: in rural medicine, early recognition and prompt stabilisation often determine survival.

Managing such high-stakes cases was, indeed, a task — but the exposure was priceless. My clinical judgement sharpened, and my confidence grew.

The Disease Burden on the Ground

During my posting, I witnessed a complex mix of health problems. Seasonal fevers, gastrointestinal infections, respiratory illness, anaemia, nutritional deficiencies, and maternal and child health concerns were among the most common presentations.

My OPD was consistently filled with complaints of musculoskeletal pain. The constant bending, harvesting, and heavy lifting inherent to farming take a massive toll on the body, and managing these strain injuries with repeated, quick-fix painkillers felt like treating the symptom while ignoring the cause.

I was struck by how many patients presented with unmanaged hypertension and diabetes mellitus. These are no longer "urban lifestyle diseases" — NFHS-5 data show hypertension prevalence in rural India at around 21%, rising steeply with age, with diabetes and hypertension increasingly clustering together across both rural and urban populations [1].

The greatest challenge was limited access to timely, specialised healthcare. Financial constraints, lack of awareness, and delayed health-seeking behaviour often meant patients presented only in advanced stages of disease.

Rethinking Relief: Beyond the Quick Fix

Many rural patients place great trust in allopathic medicine, often asking for instant pain-relief injections and strong, fast-acting pills. In rural areas, health is tied directly to daily survival — a single day off can mean losing a day's wages.

A middle-aged woman who worked in the fields came in for the fourth time in two months with low back pain, each time requesting "the injection" that gave her a day or two of relief before she returned to work. Instead of another shot, we tried something different: locally administered Snehana (therapeutic oiling) and Swedana (localised steam fomentation) at the PHC, alongside simple posture and rest advice she could realistically follow between farming cycles. Her relief lasted appreciably longer than with analgesics alone, and she returned weeks later — not with pain, but to ask if her husband could try the same.

While the pressure to return to work drives heavy reliance on allopathic drugs, it also reveals a real opportunity for education at the rural level.

Ayurveda's classical texts describe Swasthavritta — the science of preserving health through daily and seasonal regimen — as being just as central to medicine as the treatment of disease itself. Acharya Charaka opens the Sutrasthana of the Charaka Samhita with an entire discussion on Dinacharya (daily regimen) and Ritucharya (seasonal regimen), precisely because prevention was considered inseparable from cure [2]. Introducing simple interventions like Snehana and Swedana directly at the PHC level — procedures the Ashtanga Hridaya describes in detail as Purvakarma (preparatory measures) valuable for relieving Vata-predominant musculoskeletal pain [3] — showed me that traditional therapies can offer durable relief without adding to the burden of long-term pharmaceutical dependence.

A Golden Opportunity: The Role of ASHA Workers

We have a genuine opportunity to bridge this gap with the help of ASHA workers. These health workers can regularly teach the pillars of Swasthavritta (preventive medicine), emphasising the importance of aahar (diet). They can conduct dietary counselling around iron-rich, locally available foods for anaemia patients, and introduce Garbhasanskar — the holistic prenatal care and lifestyle practices described in both the Charaka and Sushruta Samhitas — to expecting mothers [4, 5]. Garbhasanskar aligns beautifully with the National Rural Health Mission's (NRHM) focus on reducing maternal and infant mortality through culturally acceptable, holistic prenatal counselling [6].

In this way, we can gradually shift the community's mindset toward trusting traditional, lifestyle-based medicine alongside modern care.

Closing Reflection

The best part of the entire posting was the genuine respect, warmth, and gratitude I received from the rural community. It gave me a sense of job satisfaction that money cannot buy — in their eyes, this profession carries deep reverence, and the physician is the ultimate saviour.

The rural health posting was not just a mandatory rotation; it reshaped my understanding of equity, resourcefulness, and the true meaning of primary healthcare. It transformed me from a doctor who only treats symptoms into the confident healer I always hoped to become.

Dr Mahek Jamadar

MBBS Intern · Rural Health Rotation
